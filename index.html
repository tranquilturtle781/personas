THE INTERFACE: ELEVATOR CONTROL PANEL

In a nutshell, an elevator control panel provide users with the ability to partially control an elevator.

This particular interface is located in the main elevator of the CIT at Brown University. It has buttons that allow users to set their desired destination, operate the doors, and request assistance in the event of an emergency.

This panel also has the added functionality of a ID card reader, giving users the ability to gain access to restricted floors if they have been given permission.

THE USERS

Observing

  • Some users quickly press the buttons they need, but others, possibly newer users, clearly hesitate while looking for the buttons to select
  • The first floor rear button is rarely used
  • The "door open" button is a key feature, used nearly as often as the floor selections
  • Card swipes, when used, often required multiple attempts to achieve the desired outcome

Interview Questions

  1. Which buttons do you use most often?
  2. What function are you using this interface for today? Is this how you typically use it?
  3. Have you used the card swipe? If so, for what purpose?
  4. Have you ever had an experience using this panel that confused or frustrated you? What specifically caused this?
  5. Is there any specific element in this panel that confuses you, or that you have seen confuse others? Why?
INTERVIEW TAKEAWAYS
LABELS
  • There is a preference for labels on the buttons themselves rather than beside them, so that users can find the correct button more easily
DOOR CLOSE/OPEN
  • These buttons are used in a time-sensitive manner to help out others, and some felt that when they were in a rush, it was unclear that the “front” and “rear” labels referred to both of the two buttons directly below them
  • Users wondered if there was a difference in function between the front and rear close buttons due to their differing icons
CARD SWIPING
  • The interface lacks any description of the card swipe requirement - even most users who have used the card swipe function couldn't clearly stated the times when a swipe was needed.
  • Users noted having difficulty in the past with not knowing whether to swipe before or after selecting a floor
EMERGENCY USE
  • There are three separate buttons that relate to a request for assistance. Users expressed confusion about which of the three they should press if an emergency situation occurred
PERSONAS

Friendly Fiona

Friendly Fiona doesn't come to the CIT very often, but when she does, she tries to help others out. The elevators are busy, and if she sees someone rushing to make the elevator, she tries to hold the door for them. But, she struggles with the buttons and ends up letting the door close in their face.

Friendly Fiona represents the inexperienced users that don't use the CIT elevator often. She illustrates the struggles users shared regarding unintuitive buttons.

Tired TA Tom

Tom is a teaching assistant who holds TA hours in the CIT. He is also a busy student who is always running late! Tom has used this elevator several times per week for the last year, but he struggles with the card swipe nearly every time he uses the elevator after hours (and he doesn't even know what time "after hours" starts!).

Tom exemplifies the users that struggle with the card swiping element of this interface due a lack of clear usage instructions.

THE USER JOURNEY

TA Tom is late for his hours in the CIT. He needs to get to room 502 as quickly as possible!

After running across campus, he reaches the elevator in the CIT and presses the up button (hurry!)

The elevator arrives and Tom steps inside. He hits floor 5 and waits, thinking he doesn't need to swipe this early.

The elevator doesn't move and the doors reopen!

Tom tries to press floor 5 then swipe his card, but the light on the card reader stays red.

He tries swiping his card the other way around. The light turns green, but the elevator doesn't move.

Tom is frustrated. He has held hours in the CIT for over a year, and he still can't remember how to swipe correctly!

He finally tries swiping first, then pressing floor 5.

The elevator starts moving up!

Tom finally makes it to his hours, flustered and late. Five students are already waiting for his help.
